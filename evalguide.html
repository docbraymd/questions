Program Evaluation & Strategic Questioning Guide

Master the Art of Investigating Residency Programs - For IMGs in Internal Medicine
The residency interview is a two-way street. While programs evaluate you, you must strategically evaluate them. This guide equips International Medical Graduates with a comprehensive framework for investigating programs before, during, and after interviews. Remember: asking the right questions to the right people at the right time can make the difference between matching at a program that supports your success versus one that doesn't.

Red Flags to Identify Before Interviewing

  • ACGME citations or probation in recent years
  • Board pass rates significantly below national average (< 80%)
  • High resident attrition rates or multiple unfilled positions
  • Recent major leadership changes or program restructuring
  • No clear information about IMG support or visa sponsorship
  • Vague or missing fellowship/career outcome data
Program Directors and Associate Program Directors oversee the strategic vision and structure of the program. Your questions should demonstrate deep interest in program philosophy, outcomes, and systemic support. Avoid basic factual questions - focus on nuanced inquiries that show you've done your research.

üéì Program Vision & Educational Philosophy

Strategic Question:

"Beyond what's on your website, could you elaborate on any significant changes or innovations anticipated in the program's curriculum over the next 3-5 years, particularly those driven by resident feedback or evolving healthcare needs?"

Why ask this: Probes adaptability, forward-thinking, and responsiveness to feedback. Look for concrete examples of past changes and clear vision for the future.
Strategic Question:

"How does the program balance service obligations with educational opportunities, and what mechanisms ensure residents aren't just workforce but are truly learning?"

Listen for: Specific protections for educational time, clear delineation between service and learning, examples of how education is prioritized.

üìà Career Outcomes & Fellowship Success

Strategic Question:

"Could you share data on the percentage of residents who pursue fellowships versus general practice, and the specific destinations of recent graduates? Are there subspecialties where your residents consistently match?"

Key insight: Programs proud of outcomes will share specific data. Vague answers may indicate poor fellowship match rates. For IMGs, this is crucial for visa and career planning.
Follow-up if positive:

"That's impressive. What aspects of your program do you think contribute most to this fellowship success?"

Follow-up if concerning: "What support systems are in place for residents applying to competitive fellowships?"

üî¨ Research Infrastructure & Scholarly Activity

Strategic Question:

"What resources and protected time are available for resident research? Specifically, how accessible are biostatistics support and data science resources?"

What you're really asking: Is research genuinely supported or just lip service? Look for specific resources, dedicated time, and clear pathways to publication.
Advanced Question:

"If a resident has an idea for a retrospective study, what's the typical timeline from conception to publication, and what institutional support exists?"

Red flag: If they can't give specifics or say "residents can do research if they want," there's likely minimal support.

‚öñÔ∏è Program Governance & Quality Improvement

ACGME-Aligned Question:

"Could you explain how your Program Evaluation Committee functions and provide an example of a recent program improvement that resulted from PEC recommendations?"

Why this matters: ACGME requires PEC with resident participation. Concrete examples show genuine self-improvement culture.
Clinical Competency Question:

"How does the Clinical Competency Committee provide actionable feedback to residents, and how is this integrated into individualized learning plans?"

Green flag: Clear process, regular meetings, specific examples of how feedback leads to resident improvement.

üí∞ Resources & Financial Support

Practical Question:

"What funding is available for board preparation, conference attendance, and publication fees? Are these separate from or part of the CME allowance?"

Listen for: Specific dollar amounts, clear policies, coverage of board exam fees. Publication support shows commitment to academic development.

Reading Between the Lines with PD Responses

  • Defensive responses to reasonable questions suggest problematic culture
  • Vague generalities instead of specifics indicate lack of structure
  • Blaming residents for problems reveals unsupportive environment
  • Concrete examples of improvements show responsive leadership
  • Enthusiasm about resident success indicates genuine investment
Core faculty and Subspecialty Education Coordinators offer granular insights into clinical training, mentorship, and day-to-day educational experiences. They're your best source for understanding the quality of teaching and subspecialty exposure.

üè• Clinical Training & Educational Methods

Elective Flexibility Question:

"How much flexibility do residents truly have in selecting electives, and how does the program ensure robust outpatient experience for those interested in subspecialties like cardiology or rheumatology?"

Key insight: ACGME requires elective time. The question is whether it's truly flexible or just on paper.
Teaching Philosophy Question:

"How do you balance autonomy with supervision to ensure residents are learning while maintaining patient safety?"

What to assess: Clear graduated responsibility model, availability for questions, teaching during patient care.

ü§ù Mentorship & Career Development

Mentorship Structure Question:

"Could you describe the mentorship model within your subspecialty? Are residents assigned mentors or do they choose them? How often do they typically meet?"

Green flags: Formal mentorship program, regular meetings, faculty enthusiasm about mentoring.
Research Opportunities Question:

"What research projects are currently available for residents interested in [specific subspecialty]? How do residents typically get involved?"

Follow-up: "Have any residents recently presented or published work from your department?"

üìù Feedback & Evaluation Culture

Feedback Process Question:

"How is feedback typically provided to residents on your service? Can you give an example of how you help a struggling resident improve?"

Listen for: Specific feedback methods, constructive approach, patience with learning curves.
Teaching Opportunities Question:

"What opportunities exist for residents to develop their own teaching skills? Is there formal training for residents who supervise medical students?"

Why ask: Teaching experience is valuable for fellowship applications and academic careers.
Current residents provide the unfiltered truth about program culture, workload, and support systems. These conversations often happen in informal settings where residents feel more comfortable being candid. Pay attention to both what they say and their body language.

üèÉ Daily Life & Work Reality

Schedule Reality Check:

"What does a typical week actually look like at your PGY level? How often are you here past official hours?"

Red flags: Exhausted appearance, avoiding eye contact, "It's fine" with no enthusiasm, mentions of frequent duty hour violations.
Call Schedule Question:

"How is the call schedule structured, and is there adequate backup when you need help? Have you ever felt unsafe or unsupported overnight?"

What you need to know: Frequency, home vs. in-house, availability of seniors/attendings, actual vs. stated backup support.
Work-Life Balance Question:

"What do residents do for fun here? Is there actually time for life outside the hospital?"

Green flags: Specific examples of activities, genuine friendships, protected time off, respect for wellness.

üè¢ Program Culture & Support Systems

The Truth Question:

"Looking back, would you choose this program again? What would you tell your younger self about training here?"

This reveals: Overall satisfaction, major regrets, hidden challenges, true program strengths.
Support System Question:

"Have you seen the program support residents going through difficult times - academically or personally? How was that handled?"

Critical for IMGs: You're far from home - you need to know the program will have your back.
Leadership Responsiveness:

"When residents have concerns or suggestions, does anything actually change? Can you give an example?"

Listen for: Specific examples of changes, or frustrated sighs and "they say they listen but..."

üåç Diversity & IMG Experience

IMG Integration Question:

"Which medical schools and countries are represented here? How well do IMGs integrate into the program culture?"

Why this matters: Programs with many IMGs likely have better support systems for international graduates.
Hidden Challenges Question:

"What's something about this program that surprised you after matching - good or bad?"

This uncovers: Issues not mentioned in official presentations, pleasant surprises, reality vs. marketing.

Reading Resident Body Language & Subtext

  • Exhausted appearance + "It's manageable" = concerning workload
  • Looking at each other before answering = there's a story there
  • Vague positives without specifics = lack of genuine enthusiasm
  • Quick subject changes = avoiding uncomfortable truths
  • Genuine smiles and specific examples = authentic satisfaction

üö® Questions That Kill Your Candidacy

Never Ask These Questions

  • "What are your program's strengths?" (Should know from website)
  • "How many residents do you have?" (Basic fact - looks lazy)
  • "Do you have a fellowship in [X]?" (Easily researched)
  • "What rotations do you offer?" (Listed online)
  • "Are you ACGME accredited?" (If interviewing, they are)
  • "What's the salary?" (Unless in benefits session)
  • "How many vacation days?" (Save for coordinator)
  • "Can I moonlight from day one?" (Shows wrong priorities)

üé≠ Deceptive Program Tactics

How Programs Hide Problems

  • "We're like a family" - Often means boundary issues, guilt-based overtime
  • "Autonomy from day one" - Could mean inadequate supervision
  • "Flexible curriculum" - Might mean disorganized, no structure
  • "Work hard, play hard" - Usually just means work hard
  • "Resident-run service" - Potentially means attendings uninvolved
  • "Great location" - Deflecting from program weaknesses
As an IMG, you face unique challenges in the match process. Your international experience is an asset, but you must strategically position yourself and carefully evaluate programs for IMG-friendliness. Here's how to maximize your investigation strategy.

üåê IMG-Specific Questions to Ask

Visa Support Question (to PD/Coordinator):

"I noticed you sponsor [J-1/H-1B] visas. How does the program support residents through the visa process, and are there any restrictions on electives or away rotations for visa holders?"

Critical info: Some programs limit visa holders' options. Get clarity on Conrad 30 waivers if relevant.
IMG Success Question (to PD):

"How have your international medical graduates performed in terms of board passage and fellowship placement compared to the overall resident cohort?"

What you're assessing: Whether IMGs get equal support and opportunities, or just fill numbers.
Cultural Integration (to IMG Residents):

"As an IMG, what was your transition like? What support did you find most helpful, and what challenges should I be prepared for?"

Listen for: Specific support programs, mentorship from senior IMGs, cultural competency of program.

üé≠ Professional Question Phrasing

Transform Basic Questions into Sophisticated Inquiries

Instead of... Ask...
"Is the workload manageable?" "How does the program ensure resident wellness while maintaining excellent clinical training?"
"Do you support research?" "What infrastructure exists to help residents pursue scholarly activities, and how is protected time allocated?"
"What's your fellowship match rate?" "I'm interested in subspecialty training - how does the program support residents pursuing competitive fellowships?"
"Do IMGs do well here?" "How does the program support residents from diverse educational backgrounds in achieving their career goals?"
"Is there good teaching?" "How would you characterize the teaching culture here, and how do faculty balance clinical service with education?"

üéØ Closing Strong: Final Questions

Universal Closing Questions That Always Work

  • "What type of resident thrives in your program?" (Shows you want to fit)
  • "What do you personally enjoy most about working here?" (Gets them talking positively)
  • "What exciting developments are on the horizon for the program?" (Forward-thinking)
  • "Is there anything about my application you'd like me to clarify?" (Shows openness)
  • "What advice would you give an incoming intern to succeed here?" (Shows commitment)
